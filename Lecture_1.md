Kaldor facts (1961)

Per capita output grows at a constant rate in the long run.

Capital per worker grows over time at a constant rate ($k$ grows at a constant rate).

The rate of return to capital is nearly constant ($r$ is constant over time, ex: 0.05).

The ratio of capital to output is nearly constant ($\frac{K}{Y}$ is constant over time).

The output shares of capital and labor in national income are constant ($\frac{wL}{Y}$ and $\frac{rK}{Y}$ are constant over time).

The growth rate of output per worker differs substantially over countries.

The Solow Model

What is a growth model?

What is a model? A simplification of the world - by design! (Positive vs Normative).

What is a growth model? A model designed to explain aggregate production over time.

Key inputs: factors of production, production function, evolution of factors over time.

Model Overview

The Solow Growth model (Solow, 1956) is a dynamic general equilibrium macroeconomic model that aims to understand causes of income growth.

What drives long-run growth? What drives income differentials across countries?

Focuses on the role of capital accumulation and technology as potential drivers for growth.

Constant savings rate. A simplified model, but a benchmark to build on.

The Neoclassical production function is at the core of the Solow model.

Pretend there is a social planner that chooses the allocation of resources (no markets $\Rightarrow$ no prices).

Assumptions

Time is continuous (period lengths tend to zero).

Single homogeneous good that can be either invested or consumed.

Closed economy $\Rightarrow$ Savings = Investment.

No utility maximization $\Rightarrow$ saving rate is exogenous.

Population grows at an exogenous and constant rate, $n$:


$$L_t = L_0 e^{nt}$$

Technology level grows at an exogenous and constant rate (For the moment, we assume it grows at rate 0, since it makes exposition easier).

Core of the model

Just two equations:

Production function

Capital accumulation

1. The Production Function

Output, $Y_t$, is a function of a non-reproducible input (labor, $L_t$) and a reproducible factor (capital, $K_t$):


$$Y_t = F(K_t, L_t)$$

Properties:

Constant returns to scale (homogeneity of degree 1):


$$F(\lambda K, \lambda L) = \lambda F(K, L) \quad \forall \lambda > 0$$

Positive and diminishing marginal returns:


$$F_K(K,L) > 0 \quad \text{and} \quad F_L(K,L) > 0 \quad \forall K>0, \forall L>0$$

$$F_{KK}(K,L) < 0 \quad \text{and} \quad F_{LL}(K,L) < 0 \quad \forall K>0, \forall L>0$$


(What about $F_{KL}$?)

Inada conditions:


$$\lim_{K \rightarrow \infty} F_K(K,L) = 0 \quad \text{and} \quad \lim_{L \rightarrow \infty} F_L(K,L) = 0$$

$$\lim_{K \rightarrow 0} F_K(K,L) = \infty \quad \text{and} \quad \lim_{L \rightarrow 0} F_L(K,L) = \infty$$

Importance of these assumptions:

Condition 1 $\Rightarrow$ no rents under competitive markets (Euler theorem of homogeneous functions).

Condition 2 $\Rightarrow$ how much capital/labor is a concave problem.

Condition 3 $\Rightarrow$ (sufficient) for existence and uniqueness of steady state.

These three conditions together characterize what is known as the neoclassical production function.

An example of a neoclassical production function is the Cobb-Douglas:


$$Y = F(K,L) = K^\alpha L^{1-\alpha}, \quad 1 > \alpha > 0$$


Features of Cobb-Douglas: Constant returns to scale to capital and labor combined, decreasing returns to each factor in isolation, and smooth substitution between the factors.

2. Capital Accumulation

The increase in capital is equal to gross investment minus depreciation:


$$\dot{K}_t := \frac{dK_t}{dt} = I_t - \delta K_t$$

Closed economy assumption $\Rightarrow S_t = I_t$

No utility-maximization assumption $\Rightarrow$ arbitrary rule to choose between consumption and saving. The model assumes $S_t = s Y_t$ ($0 < s < 1$).

Therefore:


$$\dot{K}_t = s F(K_t, L_t) - \delta K_t$$

Prediction under Cobb-Douglas:
Plugging it into the capital law of motion:


$$\dot{K}_t = s A_t K_t^\alpha L_t^{1-\alpha} - \delta K_t$$


Divide by $K_t$ to obtain the growth rate of capital:


$$\frac{\dot{K}_t}{K_t} = s A_t K_t^{\alpha-1} L_t^{1-\alpha} - \delta$$


The growth rate of capital is decreasing in capital: poorer countries (in terms of capital) grow faster.

Re-writing the model in per-capita terms

Production function: From $Y_t = F(K_t, L_t)$ we arrive at $y_t = f(k_t)$
where lower-case letters stand for per capita variables and $f(k_t) := F(k_t, 1)$.

Capital accumulation: From $\dot{K}_t = s F(K_t, L_t) - \delta K_t$

The law of motion of capital-per-worker is:


$$\dot{k}_t = s f(k_t) - (\delta + n)k_t$$

Steady State: Definition

Steady state $\Rightarrow \frac{\dot{k}_t}{k_t}$ constant.
For $\gamma_k := \frac{\dot{k}_t}{k_t}$ to be constant we need $s \frac{f(k_t)}{k_t} - (\delta + n)$ to be constant.
Since $s, \delta$ and $n$ are parameters we require $\frac{f(k_t)}{k_t}$ to be constant:


$$\frac{d}{dt} \left( \frac{f(k_t)}{k_t} \right) = 0 \Rightarrow \gamma_k \left( f_k(k_t) - \frac{f(k_t)}{k_t} \right) = 0$$


The term in parenthesis will only be zero if $f(k_t)$ is linear in $k_t$. Therefore, with the neoclassical production function, the steady state requires $\gamma_k = 0$.

Using the definition of steady state ($\dot{k}_t = 0$), the steady state is characterized by:


$$s f(k^*) = (\delta + n)k^*$$


This is a single equation in one unknown that determines the per capita level of capital in the steady state, $k^*$.

Example: Cobb-Douglas


$$s (k^*)^\alpha = (\delta + n)k^* \rightarrow k^* = \left( \frac{s}{\delta + n} \right)^{\frac{1}{1-\alpha}}$$

Dynamics and Stability

Why do we get to a steady state?
Capital accumulation is determined by:
$\dot{k}_t = \underbrace{s f(k_t)}_{\text{investment}} - \underbrace{(\delta + n)k_t}_{\text{effective depreciation}}$

Depreciation: diminishes the capital stock (linear in capital).

Investment: increases the capital stock (concave in capital because output is concave, $f_{kk} < 0$).

For low levels of capital, investment dominates. So $k \uparrow$

For high levels of capital, depreciation dominates. So $k \downarrow$

The two forces offset each other at the steady state.

Stability:

A steady state is stable if capital approaches the steady state from any point close to it.

A steady state is unstable if capital moves away even when starting close.

Look at the derivative of the law of motion with respect to $K_t$:


$$\frac{\partial K_{t+1}}{\partial K_t} = 1 - \delta + s\alpha \left(\frac{AN}{K_t}\right)^{1-\alpha}$$


Derivative approaches infinity as $K_t \rightarrow 0$. For $K_t$ large, the derivative approaches $1-\delta$ (smaller than 1, the 45-degree line).

The law of motion crosses the 45-degree line exactly once from above: We have a unique positive steady state, which is globally and asymptotically stable.

Key Insights from the Solow Model

The steady state is globally and asymptotically stable. No matter the initial level $k_0 > 0$, we always converge to $k^*$.

Role of diminishing returns to capital: In Cobb-Douglas, $k^*$ is increasing in $\alpha$. With less curvature, an increase in capital generates a higher increase in output/investment.

Comparative statics:

$\uparrow s \Rightarrow \uparrow k^*$ and $\uparrow y^* \Rightarrow \uparrow k/y$. (Higher savings $\Rightarrow$ higher investment $\Rightarrow$ higher sustainable capital).

$\uparrow \delta \Rightarrow \downarrow k^*$ and $\downarrow y^* \Rightarrow \downarrow k/y$.

$\uparrow n \Rightarrow \downarrow k^*$ and $\downarrow y^* \Rightarrow \downarrow k/y$.

Why are some countries richer (per capita)?

Higher savings rate ($s$)

Lower population growth ($n$)

Lower depreciation ($\delta$)

Why do some countries grow more than others?

If all countries are in SS, the model relies on exogenous rate of technological progress.

Or, countries are at different stages of the transitional dynamics towards the steady state.

The Golden Rule

Which is the consumption level achieved in steady state?


$$c_t = f(k_t) - s f(k_t)$$


The SS condition implies $s f(k^*) = (n + \delta)k^*$. Therefore:


$$c^* = f(k^*) - (n + \delta)k^*$$


Notice that $c^*$ is not necessarily increasing with $k^*$ (and thus not necessarily increasing with $s$). It has a hump-shaped profile.

To maximize steady-state consumption $c^*(s)$, we find the golden-rule saving rate, $s^*$:


$$s^*: \frac{\partial c(s)}{\partial s} = 0 \Big|_{s=s^*}$$

$$\frac{\partial c^*(s)}{\partial s} = \frac{\partial k^*(s)}{\partial s} \left( \frac{\partial f(k^*)}{\partial k^*} - (n + \delta) \right)$$


Since $k^*$ increases with $s$, the condition for maximum is:


$$\frac{\partial f(k^*)}{\partial k^*} = n + \delta$$

Under Cobb-Douglas ($c(s) = (k^*)^\alpha - (n+\delta)k^*$):


$$\frac{\partial c}{\partial k^*} = \alpha (k^*)^{\alpha-1} - (n + \delta) = 0$$


Since $(k^*)^{\alpha-1} = \frac{n+\delta}{s}$, we find:


$$s^* = \alpha$$

Why is $s^*$ important?

Economies with $s > s^*$ are called dynamically inefficient: We could all save less and consume more! (Too many resources devoted to keeping up the high level of capital. E.g., Soviet Union '60s, China today?).

If $s < s^*$: Output is not large enough. A move to $s^*$ requires giving up consumption in the short run to build a higher capital level (e.g., Sub-Saharan Africa).

Transitional Dynamics and Conditional Convergence

The model's conclusions on long-run growth are poor (relies on exogenous sources).

However, it delivers crucial insights into transitions towards steady states:


$$\gamma_{k,t} = \frac{\dot{k}_t}{k_t} = s \frac{f(k_t)}{k_t} - (\delta + n)$$

Growth of output: $\gamma_{y,t} = \alpha \gamma_{k,t}$ (under Cobb-Douglas).

The higher the $k$, the lower the growth rate (diminishing returns).

Main Message: Diminishing returns to capital imply that growth by accumulation of capital gets eventually exhausted.

Absolute convergence: Poor economies grow faster. (Not strongly supported worldwide).

Conditional convergence: Economies grow faster the further away they are from their own steady state. (Supported by data).

Speed of Convergence (Log-linearization around steady state):


$$\gamma_{k,t} \simeq -\beta (\log k_t - \log k^*) \quad \text{with } \beta \equiv (1 - \alpha) (n + \delta) > 0$$


Solution to this differential equation:


$$\log k_t = (\log k_0 - \log k^*) e^{-\beta t} + \log k^*$$

$\beta$ is the speed of convergence.

Higher $\delta$ or $n$ increases convergence speed.

The saving rate $s$ has offsetting effects and cancels out under Cobb-Douglas.

Speed of convergence for output $y$ is the same as for capital ($\beta$).

Empirical Applications: The Empirics of the Solow Model

Mankiw, Romer, Weil (1992)

Is the Solow model consistent with the observed variation in income per capita?

Assumption: all countries are in their steady state.

Augmented with technological progress ($A_t = A_0 e^{gt}$).

Equation for income per capita in steady state:


$$\ln \frac{Y_t}{L_t} = \frac{\alpha}{1 - \alpha} \ln s - \frac{\alpha}{1 - \alpha} \ln(\delta + n + g) + \ln A_0 + gt$$

MRW estimate this via OLS for 1985 data, assuming $g$ and $\delta$ are constant across countries ($\delta=0.06, g=0.02$).

Results for basic model: Explains good amount of variation ($R^2 = 0.6$). BUT implied $\alpha \approx 0.66$ (too high compared to 0.33 in national accounts). The basic Solow model over-estimates the effect of the savings rate.

Extension: Adding Human Capital

Production function: $Y(t) = K(t)^\alpha H(t)^\beta [A(t)L(t)]^{1-\alpha-\beta}$

New steady state income equation:


$$\ln \frac{Y}{L} = gt + \frac{\alpha}{1 - \alpha} \ln s_k - \frac{\alpha}{1 - \alpha} \ln(\delta + n + g) + \ln A(0) + \frac{\beta}{1 - \alpha} \ln h^*$$

Proxy for $s_h$: fraction of teens enrolled in secondary school.

Results for extended model: $R^2 = 0.80$. Coefficients match theory better. Implied production function $Y = K^{1/3} H^{1/3} L^{1/3}$.

Conclusion: The basic model cannot account for differences, but basic model + human capital CAN.

The Indirect Approach & The Lucas Puzzle

Look at the rate of return to capital.

Lucas (1990): "Why Doesn't Capital Flow from Rich to Poor Countries?"

If markets are competitive, $r = f'(k) - \delta$. Under Cobb-Douglas, $f'(k) = \alpha y^{(\alpha-1)/\alpha}$.

A 10x difference in income per worker should imply a 100x difference in Marginal Product of Capital (MPK).

The Lucas Puzzle: Financial returns don't show such massive differences, and capital does not flow en masse to poor countries.

Explanations: Differences in effectiveness of labor ($A$)—knowledge, institutions, property rights, infrastructure. Caselli and Feyrer (2007) show that accounting for land and multiple sectors makes the MPK gap disappear completely.

Extensions

Solow with Poverty Traps

Suppose there are two technologies:

$Y_A = A K^\alpha L^{1-\alpha}$

$Y_B = B K^\alpha L^{1-\alpha}$ (where $B > A$)

To access technology B, the economy must pay a set-up cost $\Gamma$.

In per-capita terms (with cost $\gamma := \Gamma/L$):

$y_A = A k^\alpha$

$y_B = B k^\alpha - \gamma$

Government adopts B when $y_B > y_A$, which happens at threshold $\tilde{k}$:


$$\tilde{k} = \left( \frac{\gamma}{B - A} \right)^{\frac{1}{\alpha}}$$

Result: Generates a savings curve that intersects the depreciation line multiple times, creating Poverty Traps (a low-capital, low-productivity stable steady state, and a high-capital stable steady state).

This is a setting where a "Big Push" policy is needed: a government intervention coordinating technology adoption to break out of the trap, despite short-term costs.

Bonus: Uzawa’s Theorem

Why must technological change be labor-augmenting for a Balanced Growth Path (BGP)?
Uzawa (1961) Theorem: For an economy to be in a BGP:

$\gamma_y = \gamma_k = \gamma_c$

The production function must take the form $Y = F(K, AL)$ and $\frac{\dot{A}}{A} = g = \gamma_y - n$

Proof Sketch:

Guess exponential growth: $Y_t = e^{\gamma_y(t-T)}Y_T$, etc.

Capital accumulation: $(\gamma_k + \delta)K_T = e^{(\gamma_y - \gamma_k)(t-T)}Y_T - e^{(\gamma_c - \gamma_k)(t-T)}C_T$

Differentiating w.r.t $t$:


$$0 = (\gamma_y - \gamma_k)e^{(\gamma_y - \gamma_k)(t-T)}Y_T - (\gamma_c - \gamma_k)e^{(\gamma_c - \gamma_k)(t-T)}C_T$$

This implies $\gamma_y = \gamma_k = \gamma_c$.

By CRS: $\frac{Y_T}{Y_t} Y_t = F(A_T, \frac{K_T}{Y_T} Y_t, \frac{L_T}{Y_T} Y_t)$

Let $\tilde{A} = \frac{Y_t L_T}{L_t Y_T}$. Then $Y_t = F(K_t, \tilde{A}L_t)$.

Therefore, $\frac{\dot{\tilde{A}}}{\tilde{A}} = \gamma_y - n$.

Why? If capital and labor accumulate at different effective speeds, one input would essentially disappear. Factor shares must remain constant.
